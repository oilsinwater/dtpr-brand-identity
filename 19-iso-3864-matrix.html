ISO 3864 Integration Matrix

DTPR signs are placed in public spaces that already carry ISO 3864 safety signage. This matrix maps the relationship between DTPR's visual language and the ISO safety sign system — identifying where the systems align, where they need separation rules, and where conflicts must be resolved before physical deployment.

Section 1 — Color System Compatibility ISO 3864-1:2011 · DTPR Brand System

Section 2 — Shape System Compatibility ISO 3864-1 Geometric Shapes

ISO 3864-1 defines geometric shape-meaning conventions for safety signs. DTPR's hexagon unit is not in the ISO reserved shape set but must observe minimum separation to avoid visual confusion with safety shapes in co-placement scenarios.

Section 3 — Physical Co-placement Rules Spatial Guidelines for Mounted Signage

When DTPR signs share a physical mounting surface (door frame, wall, post) with ISO 3864 safety signs, specific spatial rules apply to prevent the sign systems from being visually conflated.

Horizontal Separation (Side-by-Side)

  • Minimum 150mm clear between ISO sign right edge and DTPR panel left edge
  • Applies to all ISO color categories (red, yellow, green, blue)
  • At 10m+ viewing distance, increase to 300mm

Vertical Separation (Stacked)

  • Minimum 100mm clear between ISO sign bottom edge and DTPR panel top edge
  • If DTPR is mounted above ISO sign: minimum 200mm (viewer reads top-to-bottom, must not conflate)

Preferred Placement Zones

  • Door frames (left or right, not competing with egress signs)
  • Adjacent column or pillar surfaces — separated by physical break
  • Floor-level kiosks at 0.5–1.0m height (well below ISO sign mounting at 1.8–2.4m)
  • Avoid: fire equipment mounting surfaces, emergency route walls, and areas with ISO density > 3 signs/m²

ISO 3864 Integration Rationale

  1. Why this matters for DTPR: DTPR is explicitly designed to appear in public spaces alongside existing safety infrastructure. Ignoring ISO 3864 creates legal and perception risk — a DTPR panel that looks like a safety sign (due to color proximity) could be legally challenged in some jurisdictions.
  2. The hexagon as strategic asset: DTPR's flat-top hexagon is not in the ISO 3864 reserved shape vocabulary. This is a structural advantage: it means DTPR has a legally and visually distinct shape that cannot be confused with ISO mandated/prohibited/warning shapes. This should be protected vigorously.
  3. Amber prohibition on physical signs: The prohibition on using DTPR amber (#FFC107) as a fill in physical signage is non-negotiable. The ISO hazard yellow is spectrally adjacent and would create an immediate visual conflict with warning signs in transit or industrial contexts.
  4. Chartreuse is clear: The chartreuse accent (#C6E93D) is the only DTPR brand color with no ISO equivalent. It is the safest color to use as a distinguishing mark across all deployment contexts, making it strategically valuable beyond its current CTA role.
