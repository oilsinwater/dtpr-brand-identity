11 · Voice & tone

Five principles, with what each forbids

The voice principles are principles, not rules — concrete voice samples belong in the brand book. This page covers three layers: the brand's five organizational values (from the Message Map), the two-track voice fork (status quo vs. stronger opinion), and the five voice principles themselves with what each forbids.

PROPOSED MEMO vision.dtpr.io dtpr.io docs.dtpr.io

Organizational values MEMO

From the brand foundation memo §3b (Message Map). The five values are the brand's behavioral contract — what it commits to be in every voice moment, not just what it says. Each value has a direct voice implication:

01 Openness
Open by default, in every direction
Voice: proactively discloses limitations; never hides what the standard doesn't do. Receipts over rhetoric.
02 Legibility
Visible enough to understand and question
Voice: plain language first; jargon only with definition on first use. Operationalizes the "nutrition label" metaphor.
03 Agency
Enables action, not just awareness
Voice: always closes with what the reader can do. Never ends on a disclosure without a next step.
04 Accountability
Holds itself to the same standard
Voice: DTPR's own communications are subject to the standard. A claim on the brand's surface is itself a disclosure.
05 Community
Built with and for the communities it serves
Voice: plural, not authoritative. A standard that wants citizen buy-in cannot speak from a podium.

The Message Map voice fork MEMO

The Message Map (June 2026) identifies two co-existing brand voices — a structural fork the brand book has to name and manage, not paper over. Both tracks are legitimate and serve different stakeholder contexts:

Track A — Status Quo
Trust as governance infrastructure

Positions DTPR as the neutral scaffolding for digital trust — already deployed, already working, already legible. Addresses Pragmatic Deployers and Technical Evaluators. Tone: calm, evidenced, low-risk.

Audience: Public Sector / city managers / developers.
Tagline register: "The open standard for technology transparency."

Track B — Stronger Opinion
Accountable Tech by Design

Takes a position: accountability should be engineered in from the start, not bolted on as a compliance afterthought. Addresses Social Sector and General Public. Tone: declarative, values-stating, manifesto-adjacent.

Audience: Social Sector / Vigilant Advocates / Impacted Citizens.
Tagline register: "Accountable Tech by Design" (MakeShift 2026 headline, strongest single-line essence in the system).

OPEN The brand book has to pick one primary track — or explicitly name when to use which, keyed to audience/surface. The two tracks are not mutually exclusive but they require different voice registers, and currently the brand uses them inconsistently. This is a brand strategy call, not a copy call.

Five voice principles

Principle 1

Legible before clever

Plain language first. Jargon only with definition on first use. The 35s / 49% homepage bounce is partly a jargon problem — visitors land looking for a plain answer and meet "datachain" and "envelope semantics" instead.

Forbids: headlines that name "datachain," "envelope," or "taxonomy" without bridge copy.

Principle 2

Specific before abstract

"What a sensor collects" beats "data governance." The brand should be observable in the world — a sign, a scan, a disclosure — not in the abstract.

Forbids: mission-statement prose in user-facing moments.

Principle 3

Accountable before impressive

Show what the standard asks of deployers, not what it accomplishes. The brand's credibility comes from the standard's stated requirements, not from marketing claims.

Forbids: marketing claims that read as product claims.

Principle 4

Quiet before urgent

The standard is calm because it's available; urgency belongs to the user, not the brand. The 35s / 49% homepage bounce is partly a voice problem — pages that read as marketing prose lose the technical and civic audiences.

Forbids: crisis-tone language on a transparency standard; hype tone for the AI track; marketing prose where the audience came looking for evidence.

OPEN The Message Map names two tracks: Track A (status quo / governance infrastructure) and Track B (stronger opinion / Accountable Tech by Design). Track B explicitly takes a stand — which pushes directly against the "quiet before urgent" heuristic. The brand book has to resolve this: either the restraint principle wins (and Track B is confined to specific surfaces), or Track B becomes the primary register and the principle is revised. See the Message Map fork section above.

Principle 5

Plural before authoritative

A standard that wants citizen buy-in cannot speak from a podium. The Vigilant Advocate is a primary audience — and advocates need a brand they can hand to a community without embarrassment.

Forbids: top-down institutional voice on citizen-facing surfaces.

What the brand promises — and what it cannot yet promise

Because DTPR is a transparency standard, the brand is subject to the standard. A claim on the website is itself a disclosure.

  • No marketing claims that the standard itself doesn't ask of deployers. A transparency standard cannot run paid-attention marketing.
  • No language the standard doesn't define. If "taxonomy" hasn't been defined on the page where it's used, the brand has failed the standard.
  • Receipts over rhetoric. Every claim on the surface should be linkable to a governance document, a deployment, or a piece of canonical content.

Structural incompleteness. The brand promise of "find out → feed back" cannot currently close on the site. There is no feedback portal, no published outcomes, no community health dashboard. Until those exist, the brand promise is structurally incomplete. The brand book should not write a transparency promise the site cannot deliver. — see consolidation plan, gap Q1.6.
